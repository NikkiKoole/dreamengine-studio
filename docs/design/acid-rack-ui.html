01

Three laws that fix "too big / too small"

Both failure modes are one bug: density drifting out of range. Too sparse → a few blown-up knobs that mean nothing; too dense → everything crammed under the touch floor. Hold density roughly constant across sizes and both disappear.

Law 1 · when the shape changes

Reflow between bands, scale within a band

Same aspect, bigger window → uniform-scale a fixed layout (cheap, consistent). Genuinely different aspect (phone-portrait vs desktop) → reflow at a breakpoint. Scaling across an aspect change is what forces the cram-or-overflow choice.

Law 2 · when space grows

Reveal more — never enlarge fewer

A bigger canvas gets more per screen (unfold panels, all four machines, step lanes, meters), not the same 3 knobs inflated. Three knobs filling a 4K window is the smell. Big screen = higher density, not bigger elements.

Law 3 · the hard floor

48 px hit area — visual can be smaller

Every tappable control captures ≥48 px (satisfies Apple 44pt + Material 48dp at once). The drawn knob may be 32 px with a transparent fat-finger pad — your ui.h already does this. Size earns rank by hierarchy, not by leftover room.

02

TB-303 — the six-knob soul

The identity is one horizontal row of six equal, small silver knobs — Tuning · Cutoff · Resonance · Env Mod · Decay · Accent — no visual hierarchy inside the row. That democratic sameness is the brand; don't cluster it, don't blow one knob up. Every clone (ReBirth, Phoscyon 2, ABL3, Roland Cloud / TB-03) left the row untouched and innovated only in the sequencer — the one thing hardware lacked: a visible, editable per-step grid with pitch + accent + slide + gate as separate layers.

TB-303 mono bassline · step programmer, never an XY grid

Keep sacred

  • 6 equal knobs, one row — canonical order, equal diameter.
  • Orange = hot — accented step, playhead, record-arm. One accent hue lets you drop text labels when tight.
  • Tiny numeric readout, not a big screen — LED charm.

Where you're allowed to be clever

  • The sequencer — per-step accent/slide toggles surfaced visually is the biggest win over hardware.
  • Subordinate extras (overdrive, delay, trims) — smaller, greyed, or on a second page (Phoscyon has 13 knobs but reads clean).

03

TR-808 & TR-909 — the beat is a colored grid

Both share a skeleton: per-voice knob band on top, the single row of 16 step buttons on the bottom. The signature is the step row split into four groups of four, colored red / orange / amber / white — each block is one quarter-note beat, so you eyeball the downbeats. It's the single biggest legibility win; keep it. They differ in two ways:

TR-808 — sparse

Instrument select = one 12-way rotary. Mostly one Level knob per voice (a few get tone/decay/tuning). Accent is itself a selectable "instrument". Roomy, calm top panel.

TR-909 — dense

Instrument select = push-buttons (hold Select + tap voice). A full knob cluster per voice (Kick: Level/Tune/Attack/Decay…). Busier panel; add Shuffle & Flam.

The core question — one lane at a time vs. the full grid. Hardware shows one voice's 16 steps at a time (focus, polymeter, fits a phone). Modern software converged on the full grid (rows = voices) for overview and speed — but the grid mathematically cannot hold the touch floor on a phone: 16 cells × ~48 px ≈ 900 px, and a phone is ~360–410. So make it band-dependent, exactly as Roland Cloud does (single-row default, expandable to per-voice lanes).

When 16 cells won't fit: fold, don't crush

Desktop / tablet / phone-landscape
1 × 16  ·  ~48–64px cells
Phone portrait — reflow
2 × 8  ·  ~40px cells
Tiny / lo-fi 320 — reshape
4 × 4  ·  ~93px cells

Never add columns to fit more; page or zoom instead. Keep the within-beat gap small and the between-beat gap ~2–3× larger — Gestalt proximity does the grouping for free. Comfortable row count is 4–8 voices; divide every 4 rows past that. Don't rely on hue alone (colour-blindness) — pair each beat colour with a brightness step or heavier border, meet 3:1 non-text contrast.

05

This maps onto what you already built

The external survey lands almost exactly on the model in device-adaptive-layout.md — it mostly confirms it and pins numbers to it. The three moves worth foregrounding:

The three-state strip = the fold states

Your folded → compact → expanded strip is exactly the industry "fold, don't crush" pattern. Each machine's compact state is the ReBirth module: the 2–3 macro knobs you ride + one finger-sized lane. That's what makes "all 4 at compact on iPad" work.

"One lane at a time" is validated

Your study's insight — ReBirth never shows an XY grid — is the hardware-faithful and phone-honest choice. Roland Cloud itself defaults to single-row, expands to lanes. Editor-swap-by-budget (piano roll on iPad, step programmer on phone) is precisely right.

The one gap field-note 018 flagged

The model is sound; last time it got bypassed — raw screen_w() pixel math instead of lay.h + finger units, judged done by the defect oracle. The fix these findings support: write the per-machine layout brief first (sections, priorities, footprints in fingers, arrangement per band, orientation policy) and diff the built cart against it — density is a design call the ui-audit sweep structurally can't see.
